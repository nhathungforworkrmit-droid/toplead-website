HR Career

HR Pathway? Why Not?

Nobody grows up wanting to work in HR.

Children say doctor, pilot, footballer. Nobody says "the person who builds the team." Yet research shows most HR professionals never planned this path — they fell into it, then refused to leave (SHRM 2025).

Why? What separates those who choose this work from those who dismiss it? The ones who walk away see cost. Paperwork, policies, the conversations nobody volunteers for. They want a clean ladder and a predictable title. Fair enough — but they mistake difficulty for insignificance.

The ones who stay see leverage. They know that 40% of HR professionals enter the field driven by organisational development, not obligation, and only 7% treat it as a paycheck (BambooHR 2026). They understand a single decision — who you hire, who you develop, who you keep — compounds across an entire company. Culture is the one asset competitors cannot copy, and 97% of HR leaders now treat it as their top priority (Gartner 2025).

That is the real divide. Not soft versus hard. Not safe versus risky. It is whether you see people as a cost to manage, or the engine of everything that follows. The accidental profession became intentional the moment its people realised that influence beats authority, and impact outlasts a title.

So decide deliberately. The team you build will remember what you chose.

BambooHR (2026) The Definitive Guide to HR Career Growth. · Gartner (2025), cited in Compono (2025) Full Research: Latest HR & Talent Statistics (2025). · SHRM (2025) Breaking into HR Without Official Experience.

Sustainability

It's Not Only a Project — It Is a Mindset

I used to think sustainability was something you could prove. A project. A certificate. A handshake from someone who mattered. Then I realised I had it wrong from the start.

Sustainability isn't something you win in a competition. It's something you choose — quietly, consistently — in every decision that has no audience and no scorecard. And that realisation is exactly what shaped how I built my company. No upfront fee. No charge until the candidate passes probation. Payment only when the result is real — when the person placed actually fits, actually stays, actually contributes. Most in the industry avoid this model because it's uncomfortable. You carry the risk. You absorb the uncertainty. But that discomfort is precisely the point — because sustainability only means something when it costs you something to keep it.

Winning that contest didn't teach me what sustainability looks like on a certificate. It taught me what it has to look like in practice: every hire, every client, every decision that no one grades but you. The standard doesn't live on the wall. It lives in how you operate when no one is watching.

Recruitment Model

The Probation Period Is The Real Interview

Anyone can perform for 30 minutes across a table. The question is who they are on day 60.

Research tells the story:

  • 46% of new hires fail within their first 18 months.
  • 82% of hiring managers had already seen the signs before it happened (TriNet 2026). The warning was there. The accountability wasn't.

This is the gap that built TopLead's model from the ground up. No fee at placement. Payment only after the candidate clears probation — because that's when the hire is actually proven. The agency carries the risk until the result is real.

Most agencies won't offer this. Not because it's impossible, but because it demands something uncomfortable: skin in the game, long after the handshake. Accountability in recruitment isn't a policy. It's a position. It means your success is structurally tied to your client's — not separated from it the moment the contract is signed.

Companies lose 20% of new hires within the first 45 days (Reworked 2026). That number doesn't move until the people responsible for placing them are still responsible after the placement.

The probation period reveals everything the interview never could. The only question is whether your recruitment partner is still watching when it does.

Incentives

Recruitment Doesn't Have a Talent Problem. It Has an Incentive Problem.

Hiring has never been cheap. The average cost per hire now exceeds $5,400, and filling a single non-executive role takes a median of 44 days (SHRM 2025).

Yet in many recruitment models, one side is rewarded the moment an offer is signed. The other only finds out weeks — or months — later whether the hiring decision actually paid off.

"That's not a hiring problem. It's an incentive problem."

At the end of the day, every system produces the behaviour it rewards. If success is measured by placements, then expect more placements. If success is measured by successful hires, then every decision — from sourcing to screening to follow-up — will start looking completely different.

The best recruitment models don't just find candidates. They can actually make accountability last as long as the hiring decision itself.

Candidates

Hesitation Costs More Than You Think

The most common reason talented people miss opportunities isn't a lack of skill — it's hesitation.

  • The wait for the "right moment."
  • The instinct to stay silent until they feel 100% ready.
  • The polite decision to follow up "later."

In today's competitive market, delay isn't just caution, it's even forfeiture. The candidates who get placed aren't always the strongest on paper. But definitely, they're the ones making the first move — who apply before they feel fully prepared, who speak up in interviews instead of waiting to be asked, who follow through instead of waiting to be chosen.

Employers can actually read that decisiveness clearly — they can see ownership, initiative, and drive in the person: the exact qualities that separate a good hire from a forgettable one. If you're a candidate, my advice is simple: stop waiting for the perfect moment because it doesn't exist. Put yourself forward, ask the bold question, and let your proactivity speak before your CV does.

At TopLead, we don't reward the loudest applications — we match attitude and skill with the right employer. However, we can only open the door when you are willing to walk through it.

SMEs

Why SMEs Keep Losing Good Candidates to Corporations

89% of SMEs cannot match corporate salaries (Employment Hero 2024), but salary isn't why they lose.

The real gap is process. The data says that the average time from posting to hire has reached 48 days — a 65% increase in just three years (BambooHR 2026). Meanwhile, top candidates are off the market within 10 days (Movement Search & Delivery 2026).

And candidates notice: 42% withdraw from hiring processes simply because scheduling takes too long (iHire 2023). Another 93% of hiring managers themselves admit the process is slower than two years ago (Robert Half 2025).

Here's the irony: SMEs already have what today's candidates want:

  • 71% of workers say development opportunities drive job satisfaction (Gallup, cited in COSE 2025).
  • 39% rank growth potential as their top factor (Yoh 2025). Smaller companies deliver both — faster promotions, broader exposure, direct leadership access.

But most SMEs never articulate this. They post generic JDs, run slow pipelines, and lose candidates to companies that simply moved first.

Therefore, we suggest three fixes for SMEs:

  • Sell the role. What exactly will the hire own? What will they build? What will they contribute? All of it should be clear in the JD.
  • Compress your timeline. Two interview rounds. One week. A decisive offer. Show the growth path — just a clear answer to "Where does this lead in 12 months?"
  • Compete on structure. Corporations aren't winning on substance. They're winning on structure.

At TopLead, we help Vietnamese SMEs close that gap — matching the right candidates to professional roles in Sales, Marketing, Finance, HR, and Operations. We don't just send CVs. We make sure talent sees what makes your company worth joining.

Retention

Silent Turnover — The Centurial Case

The premise. Most SME owners register turnover only at the point of resignation. By then, the loss is already old. The real cost begins months earlier, when an employee is still present, still delivering the minimum, but has already disengaged. It is called silent turnover, and it does not appear on any attrition report until it is too late to prevent.

Data and tracking. Employee turnover in Vietnam has reached 24%, against an "ideal" rate of roughly 10% for a stable organization. The attrition runs highest in the exact functions SMEs rely on most: Marketing, Sales, IT, and Finance (Anphabe 2023). The disengagement that precedes it is even more costly than the exit itself. McKinsey estimates that a median-size S&P 500 company loses between $228 million and $355 million annually to disengagement and attrition, with more than half of a typical workforce disengaged at any given time (McKinsey & Company 2023). The pattern holds regardless of company size: the withdrawal is quiet, the productivity drain is continuous, and the cost compounds long before a resignation letter appears.

The root cause. These exits are rarely about salary. They trace back to a mismatch at the point of hire — wrong person, wrong expectations, or wrong cultural fit. When a business hires in a rush to fill a vacancy, it does not solve the problem. It restarts the same loop, absorbing the cost of disengagement a second time.

The implication for SMEs. It is all about hiring accuracy at the first attempt. This is why TopLead screens both sides of the match, assessing candidates for the business and assessing the business for the candidate. When alignment is genuine on both ends, engagement starts high and holds, closing the gap where silent turnover takes root.

Stop rehiring. Start growing.
